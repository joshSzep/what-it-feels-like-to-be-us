# World

## Timeframe

The events of *What It Feels Like to Be Us* take place roughly **15 years after the invention of the empathy engine** introduced in *What It Feels Like to Be You*.

In that time, the technology has moved through several stages:

1. Laboratory research
2. Controlled clinical use
3. Cultural controversy
4. Global diffusion
5. Underground proliferation
6. Mass emotional networking

The world has adapted unevenly.

Empath technology has not transformed humanity overnight. Instead, it has slowly reshaped how people think about emotional experience, privacy, and connection.

---

# Emoting

The common word for using empath technology is **emoting**.

People say things like:

- "Do you want to emote?"
- "We emoted after the argument."
- "That was a heavy emote."
- "I don't emote."

The term has become normalized, especially among younger generations.

Originally developed as a clinical empathy interface, emoting now refers to any technological sharing of emotional states.

The experience typically involves:

- temporary emotional resonance
- partial emotional overlap
- limited-duration emotional states
- no direct transfer of thoughts or memories

Participants experience the emotional tone of another person's inner state.

---

# Regulation

Governments have responded to emoting in dramatically different ways.

The world has fragmented into several regulatory philosophies.

---

## Controlled Therapeutic Use

In many countries, emoting remains legal only within tightly regulated environments.

Examples include:

- psychotherapy
- restorative justice mediation
- research programs
- specialized conflict resolution

Devices must be certified, sessions supervised, and consent strictly documented.

These nations treat emoting as a powerful psychological tool requiring professional oversight.

---

## Strict Prohibition

Some countries have banned emoting entirely.

Reasons include:

- protection of emotional privacy
- fear of psychological harm
- concerns about manipulation
- cultural resistance to technological intrusion into inner life

In these regions, possession or use of empath devices may carry severe penalties.

Despite this, underground emoting networks still exist.

---

## Experimental Acceptance

A small number of societies have embraced emoting more openly.

In these places:

- empathy education may appear in schools
- public emoting centers exist
- researchers study large-scale emotional interaction

These countries act as laboratories for the future of the technology.

---

# Black Markets

As with any powerful experience, attempts to regulate emoting have produced global underground networks.

Black market systems include:

- uncertified emoting rigs
- modified empath devices
- high-intensity emotional amplifiers
- illegal group emoting environments
- emotional piracy (non-consensual emotional capture)

These markets operate through global distribution networks and encrypted communication systems.

The parallels to drug trafficking are widely recognized.

---

# Cultural Impact

Fifteen years after its invention, emoting has changed culture in subtle but significant ways.

---

## Relationships

Some couples use emoting to resolve conflicts.

Others refuse it, believing emotional transparency can become coercive.

---

## Therapy

Empath-assisted therapy has become a recognized discipline.

Practitioners debate how much emotional sharing is healthy.

---

## Politics

Political activists experiment with emoting to communicate lived experiences.

Critics warn this may become emotional manipulation.

---

## Youth Culture

Younger generations raised alongside the technology treat emoting far more casually.

For them, emotional sharing is not shocking.

But they also develop new cultural norms around emotional boundaries.

---

# The New Frontier: Mass Emoting

The newest development in empath technology is the ability to connect **large networks of participants simultaneously**.

Instead of one-to-one emotional transfer, emotional fields can be shared across:

- dozens
- hundreds
- thousands
- eventually millions of participants

These large-scale events are often called **mass emotes**.

Participants describe the experience not as telepathy but as entering a shared emotional atmosphere.

The consequences of this capability are only beginning to be understood.

---

# The Shared Moment

The novel builds toward the first truly global mass-emoting experiment.

Participants across the world connect simultaneously.

Instead of emotional chaos, the system stabilizes.

The emotional field settles into something unexpected:

- calm
- vastness
- humility
- quiet presence

Many participants later describe the experience as **awe**.

The event becomes a turning point in how humanity understands shared emotional experience.

---

# The Deeper Question

The global emote raises a troubling possibility.

Did the technology create something new?

Or did it merely reveal a truth about human consciousness that had always existed?

This question lingers long after the machines disconnect.

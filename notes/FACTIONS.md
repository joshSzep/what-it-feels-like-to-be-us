# Factions

Various groups in the world interpret empath technology differently.

These factions influence policy, culture, and public perception.

---

# The Architects

Researchers and engineers who originally developed the empathy engine.

Many of them still work in academic or research institutions.

They tend to see the technology as a scientific breakthrough that requires careful stewardship.

Some believe the technology has already escaped meaningful control.

Matt is loosely associated with this group.

---

# Clinical Practitioners

Therapists, mediators, and psychologists who use emoting professionally.

They emphasize:

- consent
- emotional safety
- limited duration sessions
- structured emotional environments

Many practitioners support the technology but oppose large-scale uncontrolled emoting.

---

# Government Regulators

Government agencies responsible for controlling empath technology.

Their concerns include:

- psychological harm
- mass emotional manipulation
- international security implications
- emotional privacy rights

Regulators often struggle to enforce rules across international borders.

---

# The Resisters

Philosophers, journalists, activists, and civil liberty advocates who oppose emoting.

Their arguments include:

- emotional privacy is fundamental
- inner life should not become public infrastructure
- shared emotional systems can be exploited
- emotional transparency may become coercive

The Resister character in the story emerges from this faction.

---

# The Underground

Black market engineers, experimenters, and participants who operate outside regulatory systems.

They produce:

- modified emoting rigs
- high-intensity emotional amplifiers
- illegal mass-emote events

Motivations vary widely:

- curiosity
- rebellion
- emotional exploration
- profit

---

# The Spiritual Interpreters

Some religious and philosophical communities interpret emoting as evidence of deeper human interconnectedness.

They view shared emotional experience as spiritually meaningful.

These groups often organize meditative or contemplative emoting events.

The global emote may partially originate from such communities.

---

# The General Public

Most people exist somewhere between curiosity and caution.

They may have:

- tried emoting once or twice
- heard stories about intense experiences
- debated whether the technology is safe

Public opinion shifts as the technology evolves.
